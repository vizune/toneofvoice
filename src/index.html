1. ‘Be them’ for a few minutes

Think like a reader, not like a writer.

Yes, show what you know about brand and technology. But not all of it, only what they need. Put yourself in their shoes and make sure it’s absolutely relevant.

Ask yourself: what should they know, feel and do?

2. Don’t go on

Say your main point first.
Use ten words, not one hundred.
Have the guts to cut.

3. Get real
(and specific)

What we do could get complex and theoretical.
Don’t let it. Use real examples or great stories to bring brand and technology to life. Analogies and metaphors help.

And remember: focus on the benefits, not the features.

4. Be an
everyday
expert

We keep things clear. In an industry that’s trying to sound corporate, or be the cleverest person in the room, we’re refreshingly simple.

How? We avoid jargon. And we always ditch consultancy speak.

5. Prove it

Show, don’t tell.

If something’s good, it will sell itself. Don’t dress it up with adjectives like ‘amazing’ or ‘ground-breaking’. Instead, tell stories that show how amazing or ground-breaking it is.

And remember the ‘as opposed to’ test…

6. Use little
words with big personalities

Let your personality shine through. Used sparingly interesting word choices or turns of phrase can surprise people or make them smile.

‘Nothing. Nada. I killed the app, tried again, nowt.’
‘Good chatbots suck up and interpret data’
‘It’s not me mate, blame the algorithms!’

7. Get up close
and personal

A good conversation builds a connection.

Remember to ask questions. Use verbs of feeling, not thinking – ‘like’ or ‘love’, rather than ‘believe’ or ‘consider’. Use personal pronouns, like ‘I’, ‘you’ and ‘we’. And use contractions, like ‘we’re’ rather than ‘we are’.

8. Pack it
full of pace

Give your writing pace and energy:

  • Vary sentence length. Try short sentences.
  • Use punctuation to break up long sentences.
  • Use bullet points for lists
  • Use sub-headings.
  • Use verbs, not nouns.
  • Use the active, not passive voice.

9. There’s no room for doom

Keep it positive. Clients like our optimism and the sense of potential. When we write and talk about brand and technology, tell stories about the opportunities that we could create together.

10. Use common sense

What’s ‘professional’? It’s not formal or corporate. But it’s respecting clients by getting the job done, doing it brilliantly, and behaving appropriately.

Think: would I say this in a meeting? If you can answer ‘yes’, you’re in the right ballpark.
